Step 3 – Evaluate the risks

However, it is not always possible to remove the risk entirely and when this is the case, the priority is to 'reduce the risk to as low as possible'.

Reducing risk to a reasonably practicable level is the basis of health and safety. When seeking to reduce risk levels, the 'hierarchy of control' should be followed.

As we have already discussed, elimination is the most desired option as it removes the hazard from the workplace altogether and thereby the risk to health and safety.

This control measure must always be considered first as it controls the risk at source and protects everyone in the workplace.

close
If the hazard cannot be eliminated, look at ways of reducing exposure to or contact with the hazard.
close

Can you substitute the hazard with something less dangerous?

For example, consider substituting equipment, processes, materials and procedures with ones that are less hazardous.

close

Can you introduce controls that isolate or contain the hazard (for example, enclosures, guarding, safe areas) or can you add safety features to existing equipment?

A redesign of work processes should also be considered or the purchase of new equipment.

close

Have you implement safe systems of work procedures, permits, inspection and maintenance regimes?

Providing adequate information, training, systems and procedures all help to promote safe systems in the workplace.

close

PPE may be provided only after all other control measures have been considered but an element of risk still remains. PPE is then provided to minimise residual risk and to allow work processes to continue.

It is regarded as a last resort because PPE only protects the people wearing it whereas the other measures protect everyone.
